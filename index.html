Toyota: Hydrogen Fuel Cell Cooling System

In this project, my team and I worked alongside the hydrogen fuel cell division of Toyota North America. Toyota's Class 8 fuel cell truck prototype used a traditional radiator cooling loop, which was insufficient in achiving the lower operating temperature required by the hydrogen system. My team and I used thermodynamic principles and analysis to propose novel solutions, then we construced a prototype testing setup to validate our results. Pictured above is the testing setup of an evaporative cooling design which takes advantage of the fuel cell output (water) to strip heat away from the radiator.

HMC Research Lab: Graphene Lubrication

In this project, I worked with Professor Gordon Krauss of HMC to developed a stable formula of machine and motor oil containing gas-phase synthesized grapheen (GSG) as an anti-wear additive, resulting in a 16% reduction in wear compared to the leading synthetic motor oil. I performed tribo-analysis of new formulations by conducting wear tests with a Universal Micro-Tribometer II and measuring the volume of missing mateiral with a 3D white light interferometer.

Previously, GSG had been shown to reduce wear between sliding metal surfaces, but only if the GSG is able to remain dispersed within the fluid and get entrained between the contact patches. Initially, the GSG would not remain dispersed in the base oil, so I researched chemicals and conducted extensive testing to determine a formula that could keep the graphene dispersed.

Meggitt Engine Systems: High Temp Applications

Jet engines use sensors called Linear Variable Differential Transformers (LVDT) for measuring displacement (for example in determining the open/close position of a valve). Any external magnetic fields can disrupt the sensitive readings coming from the LVDT, so it is imperative to shield out unwanted magnetic disturbances that can be random and strong up in the atmosphere.

In this project, I researched and developed means for protecting LVDTs electromagnetic disturbances in aerospace conditions. This was done using Helmholtz coils and strong permanent magnets to generate magnetic fields, then a hall effect sensor placed within varying types of metal encasings to measure the permeability of each metal to magnetic flux.

Grip Enhancer for Sports

This project was part of my New Product Development class to understand and apply principles of entrepreneurship. This involved conducting market research, identifying consumer pain areas, and developing prototypes of a patentable product.

My specific product was aimed to reduce the hand pain associated with sports where grip and feel is essential. This includes tennis, weight-lifting, golf, and more. I designed the body of the product to use a polyester 2 way stretch to allow for vertical movement of the hand but rigidity in the horizontal direction. I used foam inserts are strategic pain points to relieve and redistribute pressure.
